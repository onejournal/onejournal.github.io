Discovery of Hardest glass by Chinese scientists.

Chinese scientists claim to have developed world's strongest glass that as hard as diamond

Carbon is one of the most fascinating elements due to its structurally diverse allotropic forms stemming from its bonding varieties (sp, sp2, and sp3), thus exploring new forms of carbon has always been the eternal theme of scientific research. Herein, chinese reearchers achieve starnge form amorphous carbon materials with high fraction of sp3 bonding recovered from compression of fullerene C60 under high pressure and high temperature. Analysing their photoluminescence and absorption spectra demonstrates that they are semiconducting with a bandgap range of 1.5-2.2 eV, comparable to that of widely used amorphous silicon. Furthermore ,comprehensive mechanical tests demonstrate that the resultant synthesized amorphous carbon is the hardest and strongest amorphous material known so far, which can scratch diamond crystal and approach its strength. The produced amorphous carbon materials combine outstanding mechanical and electronic properties, and may potentially be used in photovoltaic applications requiring exceptional strength and wear resistance.

the scientists present a systematic study of the behavior of C60 fullerene at a pressure of 25 GPa and different temperature. Different techniques as scripted in the original research paper were : X-ray diffraction (XRD), Raman spectroscopy, high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM), and electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS), resulting in increase in sp3 hybridised carbon fraction with increase of the synthesis temperature,reaching a maximum bracket of 69-94%

In order to assure the reliability of the obtained results, researchers also induced Different hardness measurement methods including Knoop (HK), Vickers (HV) and nanoindentation hardness (HN) together with uniaxial compressive strength test leading to the result that the synthesized AM-III bulk material is the hardest and strongest AM material known to date. In addition, unlike insulator diamond, these AM carbon materials are semiconducting with relatively narrow bandgaps (1.5-2.2 eV) that opens up good perspectives for using these materials in new class of photoelectric applications.
